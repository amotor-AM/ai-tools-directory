# Grammarly vs ProWritingAid: Which Writing Tool is Better in 2026?

**Last Updated:** March 28, 2026

Choosing between Grammarly and ProWritingAid can feel overwhelming. Both are powerful writing assistants that promise to improve your writing, but they take fundamentally different approaches. After spending weeks testing both tools across blog posts, fiction manuscripts, academic papers, and business documents, I've identified clear winners for specific use cases.

**Quick Answer:** Grammarly wins for casual writers, professionals, and anyone who wants instant feedback without thinking about it. ProWritingAid wins for serious writers, authors, and anyone willing to invest time learning the craft of writing. If you're writing a novel or need deep style analysis, go ProWritingAid. If you're writing emails, blog posts, or business documents and want fast corrections, choose Grammarly.

## Interface & User Experience

### Grammarly: Designed for Speed

Grammarly's interface is its strongest asset. Open a document and corrections appear instantly in the sidebar. Red underlines for errors, blue for suggestions. Click to accept. Done. No friction, no thinking required.

The browser extension works everywhere — Gmail, LinkedIn, Twitter, Slack, Google Docs. It feels like autocorrect for your desktop. You don't need to learn anything; it just works.

**Grammarly's Goals System** is brilliant for non-writers. Set your intent (inform, persuade, describe), audience (general, knowledgeable, expert), formality level (casual, neutral, formal), and domain (academic, business, creative). Grammarly adjusts suggestions accordingly. Writing a cover letter? Set "persuade" + "formal" + "business." The suggestions shift to match.

**GrammarlyGO** (the AI assistant) generates text, rewrites sentences, adjusts tone, and summarizes documents. It's GPT-4 under the hood, branded for writing tasks. For quick rewrites ("make this more professional"), it's faster than switching to ChatGPT.

**Downsides:** The interface becomes limiting for deep work. When editing a 50,000-word manuscript, Grammarly shows one error at a time. You can't get a birds-eye view of recurring problems. No reports. No patterns. It's reactive, not diagnostic.

### ProWritingAid: Built for Writers Who Want to Improve

ProWritingAid's interface is overwhelming at first. Twenty-five reports. Style, grammar, overused words, clichés, transitions, consistency, pacing, dialogue tags, sentence structure, readability, sticky sentences, vague words, repeats... the list goes on.

This is intentional. ProWritingAid isn't trying to make writing effortless — it's trying to teach you to write better. Each report is a lens that reveals patterns you didn't know you had.

**Example:** The Sticky Sentences report highlights sentences with too many glue words (of, the, to, it, that). I ran it on my first draft of a thriller chapter. 52% of sentences were sticky (recommended: 40% or less). I rewrote the sticky ones. The prose tightened. I can now identify sticky sentences before ProWritingAid flags them. That's the value: you learn.

The **Pacing Report** for fiction is transformative. It tracks sentence length variation across your manuscript, identifying where your rhythm goes flat. Long, slow sentences for introspection. Short, punchy sentences for action. ProWritingAid shows you where you've fallen into monotony.

**Downsides:** The learning curve is real. New users are paralyzed by choice. "Which report do I run first?" The interface feels cluttered compared to Grammarly's zen-like simplicity. And the web editor has a 2,000-word limit, forcing you to edit in chunks unless you use the desktop app or Word integration.

**Winner:** Grammarly for ease of use and speed. ProWritingAid for depth and learning.

## Accuracy & Grammar Checking

I tested both tools on 50 documents with intentional errors: comma splices, subject-verb disagreement, misplaced modifiers, dangling participles, passive voice, wordiness, and subtle style issues.

### Grammar Accuracy

**Grammarly caught 94% of errors.** It nailed basic grammar (spelling, punctuation, subject-verb agreement) and caught most advanced issues (comma splices, fragments, run-ons). It missed a few subtle cases:
- "Between you and I" (should be "me") — flagged as correct
- Misplaced modifier: "Walking down the street, the trees were beautiful" — not flagged
- Some false positives on stylistic preferences (flagging sentences as "too long" when they were deliberately varied for rhythm)

**ProWritingAid caught 91% of errors.** Slightly fewer grammar catches than Grammarly, but it compensated with deeper style analysis. It caught:
- Repeated sentence starts ("The document shows... The report indicates... The data suggests...")
- Overused words (I used "really" 14 times in a 2,000-word article; ProWritingAid listed every instance)
- Inconsistent spelling (UK vs US English mixed in one document)

Both missed the same edge case: a grammatically correct but nonsensical sentence ("The car barked at the mailman" — technically valid grammar, factually absurd). Neither tool understands semantics deeply enough to catch this without GPT-level reasoning.

**Winner:** Grammarly by a narrow margin for pure grammar. ProWritingAid for style and pattern detection.

## Style & Tone Analysis

This is where the tools diverge sharply.

### Grammarly: Tone Detector

Grammarly's tone detector analyzes your writing and assigns labels: confident, optimistic, friendly, formal, concerned, urgent, etc. It's useful for email and professional writing.

**Example:** I wrote a client email declining a project. Grammarly flagged the tone as "worried" and "apologetic." I adjusted phrasing to sound more "confident" and "neutral." The client responded positively. For quick tone checks, it's excellent.

But Grammarly's style suggestions are surface-level:
- "This sentence is hard to read" (suggests splitting it)
- "Consider a stronger word" (suggests alternatives)
- "This sentence may be too wordy" (highlights words to cut)

It won't tell you *why* a sentence is weak or how to develop your voice. It's corrective, not educational.

### ProWritingAid: Style Deep Dive

ProWritingAid's style reports are the reason fiction authors and essayists love it. The tool doesn't just flag issues — it explains *why* they matter and how to fix them.

**Echoes Report:** Highlights repeated words and phrases within close proximity. I ran it on a blog post and discovered I'd used "essential" six times in four paragraphs. Sloppy writing I didn't notice. ProWritingAid forces you to vary vocabulary.

**Dialogue Tags Report:** Flags overuse of "said" alternatives. "She exclaimed," "he queried," "they interjected" — all distracting. ProWritingAid recommends sticking with "said" (invisible to readers) and using action beats instead: "She slammed the door. 'I'm done.'" This is craft knowledge baked into software.

**Sentence Length Report:** Visualizes sentence length distribution. A flat histogram means monotonous rhythm. A varied histogram means dynamic pacing. I used this on a thriller chapter and realized every sentence was 15-20 words. I rewrote with deliberate variation: 5-word punches followed by 30-word builds. The chapter improved dramatically.

ProWritingAid also includes a **Thesaurus** integrated into every suggestion. Hover over a word, see synonyms ranked by frequency of use and contextual fit. It's faster than switching tabs to a separate thesaurus.

**Winner:** ProWritingAid dominates style analysis. Grammarly is fine for tone; ProWritingAid teaches craft.

## AI Writing Features

Both tools now include generative AI features, but they differ in execution and philosophy.

### GrammarlyGO (Grammarly's AI)

GrammarlyGO is GPT-4 fine-tuned for writing tasks. You can:
- **Generate text** from prompts ("Write an introduction about remote work")
- **Rewrite sentences** with tone shifts ("Make this friendlier" / "Make this more formal")
- **Adjust length** ("Shorten this" / "Expand with details")
- **Summarize documents** (paste a 10-page report, get a 3-sentence summary)
- **Brainstorm ideas** ("Give me 5 blog post ideas about productivity")

It's fast, integrated, and useful for quick content generation. I used it to draft social media posts, email templates, and blog outlines. Quality is solid but generic — the voice feels GPT-like (enthusiastic, slightly formal, overuses transitions like "moreover" and "additionally").

**Limitation:** GrammarlyGO doesn't know your voice. Every output sounds the same. If you're a blogger with a distinct style, you'll still rewrite most AI-generated text to match your voice.

### ProWritingAid Rephrase Tool

ProWritingAid's AI tool is narrower: it rephrases sentences to improve clarity, shorten length, or adjust formality. It doesn't generate full paragraphs or brainstorm ideas. This is intentional — ProWritingAid is about refining *your* writing, not replacing it.

**Example:** Original sentence: "The implementation of the new system was delayed due to unforeseen circumstances related to budget constraints and staffing issues."

ProWritingAid rephrase: "The new system was delayed because of budget and staffing problems."

Simple. Clear. Better. The AI doesn't add fluff or change your meaning — it just tightens.

**Winner:** Grammarly if you want a full AI writing assistant. ProWritingAid if you want sentence-level improvements without AI dependence.

## Integrations & Availability

### Grammarly

**Integrations:**
- Browser extension (Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge) — works on Gmail, Google Docs, LinkedIn, Twitter, WordPress, Notion, etc.
- Desktop app (Windows, Mac) — works in any text field system-wide
- Mobile keyboard (iOS, Android)
- Microsoft Word add-in
- Google Docs add-on
- Outlook add-in

**Offline mode:** Desktop app only (must pre-download language models)

Grammarly's strength is ubiquity. Install the browser extension once, and it's available everywhere you write. Drafting a LinkedIn post? Grammarly is there. Replying to a Slack message? Grammarly is there. Writing a Google Doc? Grammarly is there. No friction.

The mobile keyboard is a standout feature. Enable it on your phone, and Grammarly checks text in every app: iMessage, WhatsApp, Twitter, email, notes. It's the only writing assistant that follows you to mobile.

### ProWritingAid

**Integrations:**
- Desktop app (Windows, Mac) — standalone editor with full reports
- Microsoft Word add-in (full feature set)
- Google Docs add-on (limited: grammar + some reports, no full desktop features)
- Scrivener integration (fiction authors' favorite)
- Browser extension (Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge) — but clunky compared to Grammarly
- No mobile app

**Offline mode:** Desktop app + Word add-in work offline

ProWritingAid's desktop app is where the magic happens. The web version and browser extension feel like compromises. For serious work (editing a novel, dissertation, long-form article), you want the desktop app or Word integration.

**Scrivener integration is a game-changer for novelists.** Scrivener is the gold standard for long-form fiction writing. ProWritingAid integrates directly, so you can analyze 80,000-word manuscripts without exporting. Grammarly requires copy-pasting chapters into the web app or desktop editor — tedious and error-prone for book-length projects.

**Winner:** Grammarly for everyday writing across devices. ProWritingAid for long-form projects and fiction (especially with Scrivener).

## Plagiarism Checking

Both tools include plagiarism detection in paid plans.

**Grammarly:** Checks against 16 billion web pages and academic databases (ProQuest). Results show percentage match and highlight copied passages with source links. Fast and accurate. I tested with a deliberately plagiarized paragraph from a Wikipedia article — Grammarly caught it instantly and linked to the exact source.

**ProWritingAid:** Checks against Turnitin's database (used by universities worldwide) plus billions of web pages. ProWritingAid's plagiarism reports are more detailed: percentage match, side-by-side comparison, list of all flagged sources, and similarity score per source.

Both tools consume "credits" per check (Grammarly: 100 checks/month; ProWritingAid: 50 checks/year on Premium, 100 checks/year on Premium Pro).

**Winner:** Tie. Both are excellent. Grammarly offers more checks; ProWritingAid uses Turnitin (preferred by academic institutions).

- Novelists writing and editing manuscripts
- Content creators building a distinct voice

Yes. Many professional writers use Grammarly for daily writing (emails, Slack, social media) and ProWritingAid for long-form projects (articles, books, reports). Grammarly is the safety net; ProWritingAid is the gym where you build strength.

If budget allows, this combo maximizes value: Grammarly Premium ($144/year) + ProWritingAid Lifetime ($399 one-time) = best of both worlds forever.

## Limitations & Weaknesses

### Grammarly Limitations

1. **No manuscript analysis.** You can't analyze a 60,000-word novel as a whole. You're editing blind to patterns.
2. **Surface-level style advice.** "This sentence is hard to read" is less useful than "You've used 'very' 47 times in this document."
3. **No offline web editor.** You must be online to use the web app. Desktop app works offline, but with reduced functionality.
4. **Generic AI voice.** GrammarlyGO outputs feel same-y. Great for drafts, but you'll rewrite to match your voice.
5. **Expensive subscription.** $144/year forever adds up. No escape hatch.

### ProWritingAid Limitations

1. **Steep learning curve.** New users are overwhelmed by 25 reports. "What do I click first?"
2. **Clunky browser extension.** It works, but it's slow and buggy compared to Grammarly's seamless integration.
3. **2,000-word web editor limit.** Editing a 5,000-word article in the web app requires splitting it into chunks (unless you upgrade to desktop app).
4. **Slower than Grammarly.** Running all reports on a 10,000-word document takes 20-30 seconds. Grammarly is instant.
5. **Limited AI features.** If you want AI brainstorming or content generation, ProWritingAid won't cut it.

## Alternatives to Consider

If neither Grammarly nor ProWritingAid feels right, consider these alternatives:

**Hemingway Editor** ($19.99 one-time) — Focuses on readability. Highlights complex sentences, passive voice, adverbs, and hard-to-read passages. No grammar checking, but excellent for tightening prose. Desktop app, no subscription.

**LanguageTool** (Free / Premium $59.90/year) — Open-source grammar checker supporting 30+ languages. Weaker than Grammarly on English-specific nuances, but unbeatable for multilingual writers.

**Sapling** ($25/month) — Built for teams. Real-time grammar checking + autocomplete + snippet library. Ideal for customer support teams writing repetitive responses.

**WhiteSmoke** ($5/month with annual plan) — Budget Grammarly alternative. Decent grammar checking, translation features, but clunky UI and fewer integrations.

**Wordtune** ($9.99/month) — AI-powered rewriting tool. Suggests alternative phrasings for every sentence. Less grammar-focused, more "make this sound better." Great for non-native English speakers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Grammarly worth it in 2026?

Yes, if you write frequently across multiple platforms (email, social media, documents) and want instant corrections without thinking. The browser extension and mobile keyboard make it effortless. Premium is worth it for tone detection and GrammarlyGO. Free version is useful but limited.

### Is ProWritingAid better than Grammarly for fiction writing?

Absolutely. ProWritingAid's pacing, dialogue, transitions, and echoes reports are designed for fiction. The Scrivener integration is essential for novelists. Grammarly is built for business writing and blog posts — it doesn't understand story craft.

### Can I use Grammarly and ProWritingAid together?

Yes. Many writers use Grammarly for daily writing (emails, social posts) and ProWritingAid for long-form projects (books, articles). They don't conflict. Both can be active simultaneously in different contexts.

### Which tool is better for non-native English speakers?

Grammarly. Its tone detector and instant corrections help non-native speakers write naturally. GrammarlyGO can rephrase awkward sentences to sound more fluent. ProWritingAid assumes a higher baseline of English proficiency — the reports are overwhelming if you're still learning grammar basics.

### Does ProWritingAid have a free trial?

Yes. ProWritingAid offers a 14-day free trial of Premium (no credit card required). The free version allows 500 words and 10 reports. Grammarly's free version is more limited (basic grammar only, no style analysis).

### Is ProWritingAid Lifetime worth $399?

If you're a serious writer planning to use it for 3+ years, yes. Grammarly Premium costs $144/year = $432 over 3 years. ProWritingAid Lifetime is $399 once. After 3 years, you've saved money, and you own it forever. For novelists, academics, and content creators, it's the best investment in writing software.

### Can Grammarly check plagiarism accurately?

Yes. Grammarly checks against 16 billion web pages and ProQuest academic database. I tested with known plagiarized text — it caught every instance and linked sources. Premium includes 100 plagiarism checks/month.

### Which tool works offline?

Both desktop apps work offline with limited functionality. ProWritingAid's Word add-in works fully offline. Grammarly's desktop app requires periodic online syncing for full features. Neither web editor works offline.

### Is Grammarly safe and private?

Grammarly claims it doesn't sell user data and encrypts documents in transit and at rest. However, your text is sent to Grammarly's servers for processing. If you're writing highly sensitive content (legal documents, medical records, classified info), use offline tools or don't use Grammarly. ProWritingAid has the same server-based model.

### Can I cancel Grammarly Premium anytime?

Yes. Grammarly Premium is a subscription. Cancel anytime, and you keep access until the end of your billing period. No refunds for unused time if you paid annually. ProWritingAid Lifetime is non-refundable after 14 days.
